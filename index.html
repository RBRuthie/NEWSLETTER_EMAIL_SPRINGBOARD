Issue XXIX — Jan 1, 2020

Happy New Year! We hope you enjoyed a restful holiday break and are looking forward to a bright new
year full of health, happiness, and of course, a successful job search.

As we wave goodbye to what was a very tumultuous 2020, you’re probably wondering what, exactly, you can expect from the job market in 2021. Springboard Career Services Manager Neepa Parikh recently spoke with The Muse about what’s on the horizon for job seekers in the new year ( read the full article here!). The big takeaway? While hiring will undoubtedly look a little different than it did pre-pandemic, there are also some exciting emerging trends. Here’s what you need to know..

  • Hiring Trends Will Vary by Industry
    • Look for tech, healthcare, fintech, e-commerce, and renewable energy to grow and industries like and hospitality to continue to be slower than normal - at least until we have a widely available vaccine and loosened restrictions on travel and socialization.
  • We're Going to See More Temporary, Freelance & Contract Opportunities
    • In an uncertain economy, some companies are favoring temporary staffing solutions. Expect to see more jobs that fall into the temp or temp-to-hire category.
  • Remote Work Isn’t Going Away
    • Between the ongoing pandemic and a cultural shift in the way we think about remote work, we’ll continue to see more companies embracing work from home arrangements.
  • Expect an Expanded Focus on Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion
    • Watch for companies to invest in reworking their recruiting and hiring practices to create more opportunities for traditionally marginalized communities.
  • Look for More Automation in Hiring
    • Hiring teams are increasingly relying on automated systems to help them process the influx of applications they receive. Expect applicant tracking systems (ATS) to
      scan your resumes, chatbots to assist you with interview scheduling, and - in some cases - pre-recorded interviews to take the place of initial phone screens.
  • The Job Market Will Continue to be Competitive
    • Unemployment will likely remain high through the first part of the new year, which means more people will be looking for work. And that translates to more applicants
      for any given job posting.

With all of this in mind, now is the perfect time to think about your 2021 job search strategy. Here’s what you can do, depending on where you are in your program.

When You’re Just Starting Out
  • Do Your Research. Familiarize yourself with your new industry by seeking out podcasts, blogs, YouTube channels, and thought leaders to follow. The more you know about your new career path, the better equipped you’ll be for a successful job search when the time comes.
  • Start Building Your Network. It’s never too early to start making connections with people in your chosen field. Start by adding folks who have the types of jobs you hope to have someday, people who work at companies you’re interested in, and fellow Springboard students and alumni.
  • Identify Your Transferable Skills. Making a career pivot doesn’t mean all of your past experience has to go out the window. Take the time to identify which skills and experiences
    will be most relevant to what you’d like to do next. They’ll come in handy when it’s time to write your resume.
When You’re in the Middle
  • Get Your Resume, LinkedIn Profile, and Cover Letter Together. In this competitive market, your application materials need to be in tip-top shape. That means thoughtfully written, ATS-friendly, and rich in relevant industry keywords.
  • Continue Expanding Your Network. And keep in touch with the people you connected with at the start of your program, too. In a tighter job market, your network will be more important than ever, as they’re the people who are most likely to help you land your next job.
  • Start Browsing Job Boards to get a sense of the current opportunities in your field and take note of the types of industries and companies that are (or aren’t hiring). This will help to inform your job search strategy when it comes time to apply.
  • Reflect on Your Job Search Timeline. Finding a new job may take longer than normal in 2021 for myriad reasons. Depending on your ideal timeline, you may need to start looking for new opportunities sooner than later.
When You’re Getting Ready to Apply for Completion
  • Practice Your Interviewing Skills. A stellar resume or top-notch portfolio can only take you so far. You’ll also have to be comfortable discussing your experience and sharing specific examples to illustrate how your skills will translate into a new job. And the more you practice, the better at it you’ll be!
  • Think About What Type of Working Arrangements Will (Or Won’t!) Work for You. Take time to ask yourself: Do I want to work from home indefinitely or would I prefer to return to an office
    when it’s safe to do so? Would it be financially feasible for me to accept a temporary role or
    will I need something more permanent? Am I open to relocating?
  • Keep Your Network in the Loop. As you approach the end of your Springboard program, remember to make time to reach out to the people in your network to share the great news, let them know that you’re in the market for a new role, and, when appropriate, to ask them for help with leads, introductions, and referrals.

No matter where you are in your career journey, it’s always wise to keep an eye on market trends, continue cultivating your network, and use every resource available to you. Your Springboard support team is here (and eager!) to help. So, don’t be shy about reaching out to your career coaches to talk through your job search strategy, review your resume, or brush up on your interviewing skills.

Cheers to a promising new year!

Community Spotlight

This month’s community member is Srdjan Santic! Course Report held an interview with one of Springboard’s Machine Learning Engineering Career Track mentors, Srdjan Santic, about his background in self-learning, his career in data science, and his passion for teaching the next generation of technologists.

Srdjan holds a bachelor’s degree in economics and a master’s degree in econometrics from the
University of Belgrade. After graduation, Srdjan worked as a statistician with The Nielsen Company, a marketing research consultancy, which taught him about data analysis, data cleaning, exploring data,
and building predictive models.

When asked why he wanted to become a mentor, Srdjan replied that he’s always loved teaching and wanted to do online mentoring to contribute something back to the community. According to Srdjan, “Self-study is very hard - I started learning Python through a 12-week course from EDX and took additional courses on machine learning through EDX and Coursera. When I was doing it, I didn’t have any friends who were programmers, so I didn’t have anyone to talk (or complain) to. I felt like a program that provides student mentorship would be a great way to give back to the data science self-study
community. It’s been the most fulfilling and rewarding experience of my professional career—hands-down.”
